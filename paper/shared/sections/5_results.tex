\subsection{Main Cross-Material Results}
\label{sec:results_main}

\cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} report transfer efficiency across all five material splits for the original 3-seed main protocol. M1 and M7 are averaged over three random seeds; M8 is a single-seed diagnostic row. We report the corrected matched-encoder six-seed elastoplastic audit separately in \cref{tab:matched_encoder_audit} rather than merging protocols. We observe a clear, asymmetric pattern.

\paragraph{Gain on the elastoplastic (recoverable-rebound) split.}
On the OOD elastoplastic split, \PTA{} (M7) achieves $60.7$\% transfer (3-seed mean), compared to $46.0$\% for the matched reactive baseline M1---an absolute gain of 14.7 percentage points, or 32\% relative. Spill drops by exactly 14.7 percentage points (39.3\% vs.\ 54.0\%), and success rate (fraction of episodes exceeding 30\% transfer) rises from 50.0\% to 66.7\%. The improvement is therefore consistent across all three metrics. Given the high per-seed variance, we discuss the statistical strength of this gain in \cref{sec:results_stats} before drawing conclusions.

\paragraph{Opposite sign on persistent-perturbation splits.}
On ID sand, M1 outperforms \PTA{} by 22.2pp (64.1\% vs 41.9\%); on parameter-shifted sand variants, M1 leads by 13--22pp; and on cohesive snow, M1 leads by 13.3pp (55.9\% vs 42.6\%). These negative signs are part of the empirical finding: the same probe that helps on recoverable elastoplastic dynamics is costly on splits where the contact perturbation persists. This is the central asymmetry that the recoverable-deformation hypothesis is designed to explain (\cref{sec:results_analysis}).

\paragraph{Privileged-parameter collapse on the elastoplastic split.}
The privileged-parameter baseline M8 receives ground-truth material parameters and is otherwise matched to M7. Unlike M1 and M7, M8 was scored from a single trained seed in this evaluation; we treat its row in \cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} as diagnostic rather than as a 3-seed comparison. M8 is near M1 on ID sand (64.6\%), snow (61.1\%), and the parameter-shifted sand variants (62.4\%, 53.0\%). On OOD elastoplastic, its single scored seed collapses to exactly 0\% transfer. Diagnostic checks rule out simulator crashes (zero NaN events; M1 and M7 produce nonzero transfer on the same env state) and are consistent with residual over-correction of the elastoplastic rebound by a head shaped on sand, where rebound is negligible. In our matched setup, a policy with passive access to $(E, \nu, \rho, \dots)$ could not extrapolate from sand to elastoplastic, while \PTA{}'s in-episode probing trace did.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:results_ablation}

\cref{tab:ablation} and \cref{fig:ablation} break down \PTA{}'s elastoplastic gain by component, holding all other training pipeline elements fixed.

\paragraph{Probe execution carries signal; conditioning closes the gap.}
Removing the probing actions (M7-NoProbe) collapses transfer to 33.1\%---a 27.6pp drop from M7 Full and 12.9pp \emph{below} the reactive baseline. Removing only the belief encoding while keeping the probing phase (M7-NoBelief) preserves 46.3\% transfer, matching the reactive baseline but 14.4pp below \PTA{} Full. Spill follows the same ordering, and success rate exhibits an even sharper separation: M7 Full succeeds in 66.7\% of elastoplastic episodes, versus 33.3\% for No-Probe and 46.7\% for No-Belief.

This pattern argues against a pure wrapper-stack or model-capacity explanation for the full elastoplastic gain: No-Probe is worse than M1, and No-Belief remains near M1 rather than approaching full \PTA{}. The result instead implies that the probe-induced state shift is harmful unless the downstream policy can exploit a useful conditioning signal.

\paragraph{In this architecture, removing the probe is more damaging than removing the encoder output.}
On the elastoplastic split, removing the probe drops transfer by 27.6pp while removing only the encoder output drops it by 14.4pp. Within \PTA{}'s architecture and on this split, this is consistent with the physical probing interaction---which temporarily reshapes the particle pile and elicits an elastoplastic rebound---supplying signal that is not recoverable from passive observation alone. We do not generalize this ordering beyond the present architecture, ablation pair, and split: a different probe schedule, encoder, or material could in principle invert it.

\subsection{Statistical Reading of the Elastoplastic Gain}
\label{sec:results_stats}

With the original $n=3$ main protocol, $10$ deterministic episodes per (method, seed, split), and a per-seed transfer standard deviation of $\sim$40pp on elastoplastic, we report effect sizes and per-seed metrics rather than collapse the result into a significance claim. \cref{fig:seeds} shows that \PTA{}'s seed-mean exceeds M1's on two of the three matched seed pairs (the third is closer). The mean gain is supported by triangulation---higher mean transfer, lower mean spill (also 14.7pp), higher success rate---and by ablations where skipping the probe falls below M1 while dropping only the encoder returns to roughly M1-level transfer.

The corrected matched-encoder audit further addresses the encoder-protocol concern. The legacy random-evaluation-encoder diagnostic intentionally paired a fixed M7 policy with fresh random evaluation encoders and is not claim-bearing. Under the matched sidecar protocol, seed 42 passes corrected G2 with $96.8$\% elastoplastic transfer and zero failed episodes. We therefore treat the elastoplastic direction as strengthened but still report the near ties and high baseline variance explicitly.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations and Recoverability}
\label{sec:results_analysis}

\cref{fig:main} shows an asymmetric pattern: \PTA{} helps on elastoplastic and hurts elsewhere. Two simpler accounts must be considered. First, if the elastoplastic gain were only an OOD-distance or capacity effect, then the same wrapper should also help on the sand parameter shifts; instead Sand-Hard and Sand-Soft remain negative, No-Belief stays near M1 on elastoplastic, and No-Probe drops below M1. This weakens but does not eliminate capacity/OOD-distance explanations because our material distances are not metric-matched. Second, sand-only training may itself make probe perturbations harmful on sand-like regimes; the ID-sand loss is consistent with this and is part of the intervention-cost signal. Seed noise remains the third alternative: elastoplastic has $\sim$40pp dispersion. We therefore treat recoverability as the current organizing hypothesis, to be distinguished by matched material-distance sweeps, equal-capacity dummy probes, non-sand training sources, and direct post-probe recovery measurements.

\paragraph{Elastoplastic (recoverable rebound): gain.}
A probe displaces particles, but the displacement partially relaxes during the brief settle before the task phase, so rebound information survives in the trace while substrate disruption is largely undone. This is the regime in which the hypothesis predicts a favorable probe trade, and \PTA{} achieves the 14.7pp transfer gain.

\paragraph{Granular (ID sand and sand variants): loss.}
Granular media without cohesion rearrange irreversibly: probes push grains forward and shift the centroid in a way that does not relax. The 22.2pp loss on ID sand is the cleanest signal---the policy was trained on this exact material yet loses because probing has degraded the substrate. Sand-Hard and Sand-Soft show the same direction at similar magnitude, weakening a generic ``any-OOD'' explanation though not isolating recoverability from intra-family confounders.

\paragraph{Cohesive snow: persistent-perturbation loss.}
Cohesive snow holds together against probe perturbations but does not actively rebound before the downstream push, leaving a compacted or displaced region in this benchmark. \PTA{}'s 13.3pp snow loss is therefore the boundary case anticipated in \cref{sec:experiments}: it refines the hypothesis toward post-perturbation recoverability rather than material labels such as ``cohesive'' versus ``granular.''
