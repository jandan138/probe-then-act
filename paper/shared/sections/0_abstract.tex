The action that reveals hidden dynamics can also change the state a robot must control. We study this information--intervention tradeoff in contact-rich material manipulation with \PTA{}, a deliberately minimal probe-and-act policy: a fixed interaction elicits a material response, a paired encoder summarizes the trace, and a residual policy acts from the perturbed state. In a Genesis edge-push benchmark, all learned policies train on sand and transfer zero-shot to shifted sand, snow, and elastoplastic media. The result is asymmetric. \PTA{} improves elastoplastic transfer by 14.7pp over reactive PPO (60.7\% vs.\ 46.0\%) and also improves spill and success, while losing by 13--22pp on ID sand, shifted sand, and snow. A matched-encoder audit strengthens the elastoplastic direction: six matched M7 seeds average 93.5\% transfer versus 39.4\% for six reactive seeds, with four wins and two near ties. Ablations show that removing the probe falls below the reactive baseline, and removing only the encoder output returns near baseline. We frame this benchmark pattern as a recoverability hypothesis: probing can help when the induced disturbance relaxes before control matters, and can hurt when it persists.
