The action that reveals hidden dynamics also creates the state a robot must control. We study this information--intervention tradeoff in contact-rich material manipulation with \PTA{}, a controlled probe-and-act policy: a fixed interaction elicits a material response, a paired encoder summarizes the trace, and a residual policy acts from the perturbed state. In a Genesis edge-push benchmark, all learned policies train on sand and transfer zero-shot to shifted sand, snow, and elastoplastic media. The empirical signature is asymmetric. \PTA{} improves elastoplastic transfer by 14.7pp over reactive PPO (60.7\% vs.\ 46.0\%) and also improves spill and success, while paying a 13--22pp intervention cost on ID sand, shifted sand, and snow. A matched-encoder audit strengthens the elastoplastic direction: six matched M7 seeds average 93.5\% transfer versus 39.4\% for six reactive seeds, with four wins and two near ties. Ablations show that removing the probe falls below the reactive baseline, and removing only the encoder output returns near baseline. We organize this benchmark pattern as a recoverability hypothesis: probing is advantageous when the induced disturbance relaxes before control matters, and costly when it persists.
