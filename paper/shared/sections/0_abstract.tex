The action that reveals hidden dynamics can also change the state a robot must control. We study this information--intervention tradeoff in contact-rich material manipulation using \PTA{}, a deliberately minimal experimental probe: a fixed interaction elicits a material response, an encoder summarizes the proprioceptive and simulator-derived aggregate-particle trace, and a residual policy acts from the perturbed state. On a Genesis edge-push benchmark spanning sand, snow, and elastoplastic media, all learned policies train on sand and transfer zero-shot. The central observation is an asymmetric sign pattern. On OOD elastoplastic, \PTA{} improves transfer efficiency by 14.7 percentage points (60.7\% vs.\ 46.0\%), reduces spillage by 14.7 points (39.3\% vs.\ 54.0\%), and raises success by 16.7 points over reactive PPO. Matched ablations show that removing the probe falls below the reactive baseline, while removing only the encoder output returns to roughly baseline transfer and remains below full \PTA{}. On ID sand, two sand parameter shifts, and snow, the same probe-conditioned policy underperforms by 13--22 points. We organize this pattern through a falsifiable recoverability hypothesis: active context acquisition is net useful when the probe-induced state shift relaxes on the task timescale, and costly when the intervention leaves persistent disruption. The claim is scoped to this diagnostic benchmark and motivates recoverability as a testable organizing variable for future material families and probe designs.
