Hidden physical dynamics make material-adaptive reinforcement learning difficult: visually similar material states can respond differently to contact. We propose \PTA{}, a deliberately minimal two-stage framework that executes a fixed probe, encodes the resulting proprioceptive and aggregate-particle trace into a latent task-conditioning vector, and conditions a residual policy on that vector. We evaluate on a Genesis edge-push benchmark spanning sand, snow, and elastoplastic media; all methods train on sand only and transfer zero-shot. The empirical picture is asymmetric. On OOD elastoplastic, \PTA{} improves transfer efficiency by 14.7 percentage points (60.7\% vs.\ 46.0\%) and reduces spillage by 14.7 percentage points (39.3\% vs.\ 54.0\%) over reactive PPO; ablations show that both the fixed probe and latent conditioning are needed for this gain. On ID sand, two sand parameter shifts, and snow, \PTA{} underperforms by 13--22 percentage points. We therefore do not claim broad cross-material robustness. The pattern supports a \emph{recoverable-response hypothesis}: probing helps when perturbations relax on the task timescale and hurts when they persist. A single-seed diagnostic privileged-parameter baseline collapses to 0\% transfer on elastoplastic, suggesting that in this sand-only setup an interaction trace can be more useful than passive material parameters. \PTA{} is best read as a diagnostic tool for studying when active context acquisition helps or hurts under hidden dynamics.
