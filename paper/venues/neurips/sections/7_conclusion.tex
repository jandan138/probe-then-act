\section{Conclusion and Limitations}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \PTA{} as a minimal experimental probe for active in-episode context acquisition under hidden dynamics. The core point is that probing is an intervention: it gives the network a response trace, but it also changes the physical state that the task policy must control. In our cross-material Genesis benchmark, this mechanism improves transfer, spill, and success on the recoverable elastoplastic OOD split in the original main protocol, and a corrected matched-encoder six-seed audit strengthens that direction (93.5\% mean M7 transfer versus 39.4\% for six reactive seeds, four wins and two near ties). The same mechanism has the opposite sign on ID sand, snow, and two sand parameter shifts. The asymmetry is the finding: negative splits expose the physical cost of probing rather than merely weakening a method claim.

We organize the pattern through a recoverability hypothesis: probing is worthwhile when the perturbation relaxes on the task timescale and costly when it leaves persistent disruption. Three concrete tests would distinguish this account from OOD-distance, capacity, and sand-training artifacts: matched recoverable/nonrecoverable materials should flip the probe sign with measured post-probe relaxation rather than material label; increasing probe amplitude or reducing settle time should turn an elastoplastic gain negative past a residual-deformation threshold; and equal-capacity dummy probes or non-sand training sources should not create a probe advantage on persistent-perturbation regimes. Failure of these directional tests would reject recoverability as the organizing explanation.\label{sec:conclusion:end}
