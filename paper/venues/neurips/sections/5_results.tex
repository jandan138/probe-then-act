\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

These results isolate active context acquisition as an information--intervention tradeoff rather than a benchmark-leaderboard exercise. The object of study is the sign pattern induced by a physical probe: where information gain dominates state perturbation, where perturbation dominates, and whether the signs match the recoverability hypothesis.

\subsection{Main Cross-Material Results}
\label{sec:results_main}

\cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} report transfer efficiency across all five material splits for the original 3-seed protocol. M1 and M7 use three seeds; M8 is a single-seed privileged-parameter row. We keep the six-seed matched-encoder elastoplastic audit separate in \cref{tab:matched_encoder_audit}. The pattern is asymmetric.

\paragraph{Gain on the elastoplastic (recoverable-rebound) split.}
On elastoplastic, \PTA{} gains 14.7pp in transfer over M1 and improves spill and success in the same direction. The metrics align, but seed variance is high (\cref{sec:results_stats}).

\paragraph{Opposite sign on persistent-perturbation splits.}
On ID sand, shifted sand, and snow, M1 leads by 13--22pp. These negative signs are part of the finding: the probe that helps on recoverable elastoplastic dynamics is costly when the contact perturbation persists. We interpret this asymmetry through recoverability in \cref{sec:results_analysis}.

\paragraph{Single-seed privileged-parameter failure on elastoplastic.}
M8 receives ground-truth material parameters but is scored from a single seed, so we treat it as a privileged-parameter stress test rather than a 3-seed comparison. It is strong on several sand-like splits yet scores 0.0\% transfer on elastoplastic. In this sand-only residual-control setup, passive parameter access did not produce the elastoplastic response captured by the probe trace.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:results_ablation}

\cref{tab:ablation} breaks down \PTA{}'s elastoplastic gain by component, holding all other training pipeline elements fixed; Appendix \cref{fig:ablation} visualizes the per-seed ranges.

\paragraph{Probe execution carries signal; conditioning closes the gap.}
Removing probing drops transfer below M1. Keeping the probe but dropping the encoder output returns to roughly M1-level transfer, well below full \PTA{}. The gain therefore appears to require both probe execution and usable conditioning, not either alone.

\subsection{Statistical Reading of the Elastoplastic Gain}
\label{sec:results_stats}

With $n=3$ seeds and $\sim$40pp elastoplastic dispersion, we report effect sizes rather than significance claims. Appendix \cref{fig:seeds} shows that \PTA{} exceeds M1 on two of three matched seed pairs (one near tie, one large gain) and drops sharply on the remaining seed. Dispersion is large, but transfer, spill, and success move together on elastoplastic, while the persistent-perturbation splits move in the opposite direction.

\input{figures/TABLE_4_matched_encoder_audit}

A separate matched-encoder audit adds a protocol-controlled check. Across six matched M7 policy seeds, M7 averages $93.5\pm7.4$\% elastoplastic transfer versus $39.4\pm47.9$\% for six reactive seeds, with a $+54.2$pp mean paired delta. Four pairs favor M7; the two reactive wins are near ties ($-1.2$pp and $-0.1$pp). The audit strengthens the elastoplastic direction without changing the 3-seed cross-split protocol.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations and Recoverability}
\label{sec:results_analysis}

\cref{fig:effect_delta} shows the recoverability-aligned sign pattern: \PTA{} gains on elastoplastic but pays costs on ID sand, Sand-Hard, Sand-Soft, and snow. This pattern separates recoverability from generic OOD robustness or capacity: if either were sufficient, the sand parameter shifts should also improve; they do not. Sand-only training may amplify probe costs on sand-like regimes, which is consistent with the intervention-cost signal rather than a contradiction. Elastoplastic dispersion remains large ($\sim$40pp), so recoverability is a working, falsifiable explanation to be tested with matched material-distance sweeps, dummy probes, non-sand training sources, and direct post-probe recovery measurements. Snow is the boundary case anticipated in \cref{sec:experiments}: cohesive in appearance, but negative under contact loading.
