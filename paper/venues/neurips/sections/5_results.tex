\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We read the results as a diagnostic of active context acquisition rather than as a leaderboard. The object of study is the sign pattern induced by a physical probe: where the information gain dominates the state perturbation, where the perturbation dominates, and whether the split-level signs match the recoverability hypothesis.

\subsection{Main Cross-Material Results}
\label{sec:results_main}

\cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} report transfer efficiency across all five material splits. M1 and M7 use three seeds; M8 is a single-seed diagnostic row. The pattern is asymmetric.

\paragraph{Gain on the elastoplastic (recoverable-rebound) split.}
On the OOD elastoplastic split, \PTA{} (M7) achieves $60.7$\% transfer versus $46.0$\% for M1, a 14.7pp gain. Spill also improves by 14.7pp (39.3\% vs.\ 54.0\%), and success rises from 50.0\% to 66.7\%. The direction aligns across metrics, but the seed variance is high (\cref{sec:results_stats}).

\paragraph{Opposite sign on persistent-perturbation splits.}
On ID sand, M1 outperforms \PTA{} by 22.2pp (64.1\% vs 41.9\%); on parameter-shifted sand variants, M1 leads by 13--22pp; and on cohesive snow, M1 leads by 13.3pp (55.9\% vs 42.6\%). These negative signs are part of the empirical finding: the same probe that helps on recoverable elastoplastic dynamics is costly on splits where the contact perturbation persists. We interpret this central asymmetry through the recoverability hypothesis in \cref{sec:results_analysis}.

\paragraph{Single-seed privileged-parameter failure on elastoplastic.}
M8 receives ground-truth material parameters but is otherwise matched to M7 and scored from a single seed, so we treat it as diagnostic. It is close to or above M1 on ID sand, snow, and Sand-Hard, trails M1 on Sand-Soft, and scores 0.0\% transfer on elastoplastic. Zero NaN events rule out simulator crashes as the direct explanation. In this sand-only residual-control setup, the privileged parameter vector did not produce a useful elastoplastic response curve, while the probe trace retained nonzero elastoplastic transfer.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:results_ablation}

\cref{tab:ablation} breaks down \PTA{}'s elastoplastic gain by component, holding all other training pipeline elements fixed; Appendix \cref{fig:ablation} visualizes the per-seed ranges.

\paragraph{Probe execution carries signal; conditioning closes the gap.}
Removing probing drops transfer to 33.1\%, 27.6pp below M7 Full and 12.9pp below M1. Keeping the probe but dropping the encoder output gives 46.3\%, essentially M1-level but 14.4pp below full \PTA{}. The ablations therefore suggest, without proving, that the elastoplastic gain depends on coupling a physical probe with usable conditioning rather than merely adding wrapper capacity.

\subsection{Statistical Reading of the Elastoplastic Gain}
\label{sec:results_stats}

With $n=3$ seeds, 10 episodes per (method, seed, split), and $\sim$40pp elastoplastic seed standard deviation, we report effect sizes and per-seed metrics rather than collapse the result into a significance claim. Appendix \cref{fig:seeds} shows that \PTA{} exceeds M1 on two matched seed pairs (one large gain, one near tie) and loses sharply on the third. The positive split is therefore high-variance, but the three reported metrics on elastoplastic move in the same direction, and the four persistent-perturbation splits move in the opposite direction in the seed means.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations and Recoverability}
\label{sec:results_analysis}

\cref{fig:effect_delta} shows the sign pattern behind the recoverability hypothesis: \PTA{} gains on elastoplastic but loses on ID sand, Sand-Hard, Sand-Soft, and snow. Two simpler accounts must be considered. First, if the elastoplastic gain were only an OOD-distance or capacity effect, then the same wrapper should also help on the sand parameter shifts; instead Sand-Hard and Sand-Soft remain negative, No-Belief stays near M1 on elastoplastic, and No-Probe drops below M1. This weakens but does not eliminate capacity/OOD-distance explanations because our material distances are not metric-matched. Second, sand-only training may itself make probe perturbations harmful on sand-like regimes; the ID-sand loss is consistent with this and is part of the intervention-cost signal, not a separate failure mode. Seed noise remains the third alternative: elastoplastic has $\sim$40pp dispersion. We therefore treat recoverability as the current organizing hypothesis, to be distinguished by matched material-distance sweeps, equal-capacity dummy probes, non-sand training sources, and direct post-probe recovery measurements. Snow is the boundary case anticipated in \cref{sec:experiments}: cohesive in appearance, but negative under contact loading, consistent with persistent compaction or displacement rather than rebound.
