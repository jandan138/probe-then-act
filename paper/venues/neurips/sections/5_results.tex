\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We read the results as a diagnostic of active context acquisition rather than as a leaderboard comparison. The key question is not whether \PTA{} dominates across materials, but whether the sign of its effect changes with the recoverability of the probe-induced perturbation.

We organize the results around three questions: (i) does active probing improve cross-material transfer, and on which materials? (\cref{sec:results_main}), (ii) which components drive the gain? (\cref{sec:results_ablation}), and (iii) does the per-split pattern align with the recoverable-response hypothesis from \cref{sec:introduction}? (\cref{sec:results_analysis}).

\subsection{Main Cross-Material Results}
\label{sec:results_main}

\cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} report transfer efficiency across all five material splits. M1 and M7 are averaged over three random seeds; M8 is a single-seed diagnostic row. We observe a clear, asymmetric pattern.

\paragraph{Gain on the elastoplastic (recoverable-rebound) split.}
On the OOD elastoplastic split, \PTA{} (M7) achieves $60.7$\% transfer (3-seed mean), compared to $46.0$\% for the matched reactive baseline M1---an absolute gain of 14.7 percentage points. Spill drops by exactly 14.7 percentage points (39.3\% vs.\ 54.0\%), and success rate (fraction of episodes exceeding 30\% transfer) rises from 50.0\% to 66.7\%. The improvement is therefore consistent across all three metrics. Given the high per-seed variance, we discuss the statistical strength of this gain in \cref{sec:results_stats} before drawing conclusions.

\paragraph{Loss on granular and within-family OOD.}
On ID sand, M1 outperforms \PTA{} by 22.2pp (64.1\% vs 41.9\%); on parameter-shifted sand variants, M1 leads by 13--22pp; and on cohesive snow, M1 leads by 13.3pp (55.9\% vs 42.6\%). \PTA{} therefore wins on exactly one of five splits. This is the central asymmetry that the recoverable-response hypothesis is designed to explain (\cref{sec:results_analysis}).

\paragraph{Privileged-parameter collapse on the elastoplastic split.}
The privileged-parameter baseline M8 receives ground-truth material parameters and is otherwise matched to M7. Unlike M1 and M7, M8 was scored from a single trained seed in this evaluation; we treat its row in \cref{tab:main_results} and \cref{fig:main} as diagnostic rather than as a 3-seed comparison. M8 is close to M1 on ID sand (64.6\% vs. 64.1\%), exceeds M1 on snow (61.1\% vs. 55.9\%) and Sand-Hard (62.4\% vs. 62.3\%), and trails M1 on Sand-Soft (53.0\% vs. 68.7\%). On OOD elastoplastic, M8 scores 0.0\% transfer in its single scored diagnostic run. Zero NaN events rule out simulator crashes as the direct explanation, but they do not by themselves isolate the mechanism; the rollout pattern is consistent with residual over-correction by a sand-shaped policy rather than with a universal failure of privileged information. We read M8 narrowly as a policy-generalization failure of \emph{this particular} privileged baseline under sand-only training, not as a general comparison between parameter access and interaction traces. Within that scope, in our matched setup a policy with passive access to $(E, \nu, \rho, \dots)$ could not extrapolate from sand to elastoplastic, while \PTA{}'s in-episode probing trace did.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:results_ablation}

\cref{tab:ablation} breaks down \PTA{}'s elastoplastic gain by component, holding all other training pipeline elements fixed; Appendix \cref{fig:ablation} visualizes the per-seed ranges.

\paragraph{Both components are necessary.}
Removing the probing actions (M7-NoProbe) collapses transfer to 33.1\%---a 27.6pp drop from M7 Full and 12.9pp \emph{below} the reactive baseline. Removing only the belief encoding while keeping the probing phase (M7-NoBelief) preserves 46.3\% transfer, matching the reactive baseline but 14.4pp below \PTA{} Full. For auxiliary metrics, M7 Full has the lowest spill and highest success, while No-Belief remains close to M1 rather than clearly below it on every metric. The ablations therefore argue against a pure wrapper-stack or model-capacity explanation: the full elastoplastic gain depends on coupling the physical probe with a usable conditioning representation. We do not generalize the relative probe-versus-encoder ordering beyond this architecture, ablation pair, and split.

\subsection{Statistical Reading of the Elastoplastic Gain}
\label{sec:results_stats}

With $n=3$ seeds, $10$ deterministic episodes per (method, seed, split), and a per-seed transfer standard deviation of $\sim$40pp on elastoplastic, classical significance tests are underpowered; we make no $p$-value claim on the 14.7pp gap. Appendix \cref{fig:seeds} shows that \PTA{} exceeds M1 on two of the three matched seed pairs: one large gain on seed 42 and one near-tie on seed 1; seed 0 is a sharp loss. The mean gain is supported by triangulation---higher mean transfer, lower mean spill (also 14.7pp), higher success rate---and by ablations that drop elastoplastic transfer to approximately the reactive baseline or below. The evidence is directional rather than statistically conclusive; a single adverse seed could narrow the gap. We release per-seed numbers and the benchmark code to make additional seeds straightforward to add.

\subsection{Per-Split Pattern and the Recoverable-Response Hypothesis}
\label{sec:results_analysis}

\cref{fig:main} shows the sign pattern behind the recoverable-response hypothesis: \PTA{} gains on elastoplastic (+14.7pp), but loses on ID sand, Sand-Hard, Sand-Soft, and snow. This is consistent with the hypothesis that probing is useful when the elicited response relaxes on the task timescale and harmful when the probe mainly leaves persistent state disruption. The evidence remains interpretive rather than identifying a law: there is only one positive material family, snow does not cleanly separate from all granular variants, and confounders such as task ease, particle density, control stability, and sand-only training are not isolated.
