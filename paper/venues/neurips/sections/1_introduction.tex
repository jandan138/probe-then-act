\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Reinforcement-learning policies deployed under hidden dynamics must often act before the latent physical context is known. A visual observation or short state history can be compatible with many transition models: two material instances may look identical while responding differently to contact, load, and motion. Standard reactive policies absorb this ambiguity into a single mapping from state to action, while context-conditioned or recurrent policies infer hidden context from experience gathered during task execution~\cite{tobin2017domain,peng2018sim2real,hausman2018learning,xie2018learning}. In embodied manipulation, the information-gathering action is itself part of the physics: the push that reveals stiffness, cohesion, or rebound also changes the state the task policy must control.

Most rapid-adaptation methods rely on information that appears naturally in the task rollout. That assumption is fragile when the informative interaction differs from the reward-seeking interaction, or when the policy must choose a strategy before the task dynamics have revealed themselves. Explicit probing offers a simple alternative: allocate a short interaction budget to query the environment, encode the response, and condition the downstream policy on it. But probing is not passive observation. It is a physical intervention that maps the original task state to a perturbed state, so the learned latent and the induced state shift jointly determine whether the downstream policy improves.

We study this tradeoff in cross-material manipulation, where hidden physical properties such as stiffness, cohesion, and flow resistance affect contact-rich control~\cite{huang2022plasticinelab,lin2022diffskill,shi2023robocook}. We operationalize explicit probing in \PTA{} (\cref{fig:pta_workflow}). We do not present \PTA{} as a competitive context-acquisition method. We use it as a minimal experimental probe, deliberately stripped of learned probing, calibrated belief, and architectural sophistication, to isolate one variable: whether an explicit information-gathering interaction helps or hurts downstream control after it has physically perturbed the material.

\paragraph{Recoverability hypothesis.}
Probing is not free: it can displace particles, break contacts, and consume task time. We organize the tradeoff with a recoverability hypothesis: probing should help when the elicited response is informative and the induced state shift relaxes on the task timescale, and should hurt when the perturbation persists. Elastoplastic media in our benchmark exhibit recoverable rebound, while granular sand rearranges irreversibly. Snow is cohesive in appearance but remains a negative case under contact loading; we therefore treat it in this benchmark as a persistent-perturbation case, consistent with compaction or displacement that does not relax before the downstream push. This hypothesis is meant to be falsifiable on additional material families and probe designs, not asserted as a settled law.

\paragraph{Empirical evaluation.}
We evaluate \PTA{} on a Genesis edge-push benchmark~\cite{genesis2024} spanning granular sand, cohesive snow, elastoplastic material with recoverable rebound, and two parameter-shifted sand variants. A scripted controller has a 55pp transfer spread across the main families, making the splits discriminative; all learned policies train on sand and evaluate zero-shot on the other four splits.

The main result is not a leaderboard win; it is a sign pattern. On elastoplastic, \PTA{} reaches $60.7$\% transfer versus $46.0$\% for reactive PPO (M1), a 14.7pp gain, with spill reduced from 54.0\% to 39.3\% and success increased from 50.0\% to 66.7\% (\cref{tab:main_results,fig:main,fig:effect_delta}). On ID sand, snow, and two sand parameter shifts, \PTA{} underperforms M1 by 13--22pp. The aligned three-metric improvement on the recoverable split and the opposite direction on four persistent-perturbation splits make the negative cases evidence about the physical cost of probing, not merely failed benchmark entries. With three seeds and high elastoplastic variance, we report effect sizes together with per-seed dispersion rather than a significance claim.

Ablations clarify the component roles on elastoplastic: removing the probe drops transfer by 27.6pp and falls below M1, while removing only the encoder output drops 14.4pp from full \PTA{} and returns to roughly M1-level transfer (\cref{tab:ablation}). A privileged-parameter baseline (M8) tests ground-truth parameter access under the same sand-only residual-control setup; its single scored seed reaches 0.0\% on elastoplastic while remaining strong elsewhere.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We contribute: (i) a physical-intervention framing of active context acquisition in contact-rich manipulation, where the experience used to infer hidden dynamics is also a task-state intervention; (ii) a controlled five-split Genesis benchmark with 55pp scripted-baseline difficulty spread, sand-only training, 3-seed M1/M7 evaluation, per-seed metrics, and ablations; (iii) an observed sign pattern in which a minimal probing mechanism improves the recoverable elastoplastic split (+14.7pp transfer, $-$14.7pp spill, +16.7pp success) and consistently underperforms on splits dominated by persistent particle rearrangement; and (iv) a recoverability hypothesis that turns the sign pattern into a testable prediction for future material families and probe designs.

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
\PTA{} uses a fixed open-loop probe, simulator-derived aggregate-particle statistics, and an uncalibrated task-conditioning latent. Our claims are scoped accordingly: \PTA{} is an experimental instrument for exposing the information--intervention tradeoff in this benchmark. The intended contribution is the phenomenon and hypothesis, with deployment robustness left as the next engineering target.
