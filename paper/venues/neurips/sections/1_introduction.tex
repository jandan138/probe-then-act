\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Reinforcement-learning policies deployed under hidden dynamics must often act before the latent physical context is known. A visual observation or short state history can be compatible with many transition models: two material instances may look identical while responding differently to contact, load, and motion. Standard reactive policies absorb this ambiguity into a single mapping from state to action, while context-conditioned or recurrent policies try to infer the hidden context from experience gathered during task execution~\cite{tobin2017domain,peng2018sim2real,hausman2018learning,xie2018learning}. The central question in this paper is whether an agent should instead spend part of the episode actively eliciting information before committing to the task policy.

Most rapid-adaptation methods rely on information that appears naturally in the task rollout. That assumption can be fragile when the informative interaction is not the same as the reward-seeking interaction, or when the policy must choose a high-level strategy before the task dynamics have revealed themselves. Explicit probing offers a simple alternative: allocate a short interaction budget to query the environment, encode the response, and condition the downstream policy on that response. The cost is equally important: probing changes the state, and in deformable or granular systems the information-gathering action can make the subsequent control problem easier or harder.

We study this tradeoff in cross-material manipulation, where hidden physical properties such as stiffness, cohesion, and flow resistance affect the outcome of contact-rich control~\cite{huang2022plasticinelab,lin2022diffskill,shi2023robocook}. We operationalize explicit probing in \PTA{} (\cref{fig:hero}). \PTA{} is deliberately minimal: the probe is a fixed open-loop trajectory, the encoder is a small MLP with mean-pooling, and the downstream controller is a PPO residual policy. It is not a new inference architecture. This minimalism is intentional: it isolates the effect of explicitly collecting context from the confounds of a more elaborate inference architecture. The active component is the budgeted in-episode data-collection phase and the learned downstream use of the elicited response; the probing action itself is not optimized as an information-gathering policy.

\PTA{} has three components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Probe phase.} The agent executes a short, fixed open-loop sequence of exploratory contacts while the task residual is held at zero. The resulting proprioceptive and aggregate-particle trace records how the environment responds to applied motion and load.
    \item \textbf{Encoder.} A small MLP with mean-pooling maps the probe trace to a latent task-conditioning vector $z \in \R^{16}$ and a learned scale $\sigma$. This probe representation is not a calibrated posterior: there is no KL regularizer, prior, or auxiliary classification loss; $z$ is whatever representation of the probe response is task-useful under the end-to-end PPO objective.
    \item \textbf{Task policy.} A joint-residual PPO policy is conditioned on $(s_t, z, \sigma)$ and emits joint-space residuals on top of a scripted edge-push base trajectory. The base trajectory sidesteps differentiable-IK coupling artifacts we observed in the simulator~\cite{genesis2024}.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Recoverability as an explanatory hypothesis.}
Probing is not free: every probe step can displace particles, break contacts, and consume task time. We use recoverability to explain when this cost may be worth paying, but we treat it throughout as a hypothesis to be tested rather than as a validated law:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Active probing should help downstream control when the response it elicits is informative and the probe-induced state change is recoverable on the task timescale; it should hurt when the same information-gathering action leaves an irreversible perturbation that the downstream policy must then repair.}
\end{quote}
In our benchmark, elastoplastic media exhibit recoverable elastic rebound, granular sand rearranges irreversibly, and cohesive snow lies between these regimes. The hypothesis therefore predicts a possible benefit on elastoplastic and a cost on granular splits, but snow remains a negative split in this setup, so the hypothesis is a lens for interpretation rather than a causal explanation established by the benchmark.

\paragraph{Empirical evaluation.}
We evaluate \PTA{} on a reproducible hidden-dynamics edge-push benchmark in Genesis~\cite{genesis2024} spanning granular sand, cohesive snow, elastoplastic material with recoverable elastic rebound, and two parameter-shifted sand variants. A scripted controller exhibits a 55-percentage-point transfer spread across the main material families, making the splits discriminative; learned-policy transfer values are reported separately below. All learned policies are trained on sand and evaluated zero-shot on the other four splits, so the conditioning mechanism must extrapolate from in-episode evidence rather than interpolate over a labeled material mixture.

The main result is mixed: \PTA{} helps on the OOD elastoplastic split but hurts on the four other splits. On elastoplastic, \PTA{} achieves $60.7$\% transfer versus $46.0$\% for the matched reactive PPO baseline (M1), a 14.7pp absolute gain, with spill reduced from 54.0\% to 39.3\% and success increased from 50.0\% to 66.7\% (\cref{tab:main_results}, \cref{fig:main}). On the four other splits, \PTA{} underperforms M1 by 13--22pp, including a 22.2pp loss on in-distribution sand. With three training seeds and high elastoplastic seed variance, we report these comparisons descriptively and make no statistical-significance claim.

Ablations on elastoplastic indicate that both pieces of the minimal intervention matter in this architecture: removing the probe drops transfer by 27.6pp, and removing the encoder output drops transfer by 14.4pp (\cref{tab:ablation}). A privileged-parameter baseline (M8) is diagnostic of naive parameter conditioning in this sand-only residual-control setup rather than a full oracle comparison; in its single scored diagnostic run it scores 0.0\% transfer on elastoplastic while remaining strong elsewhere.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three empirical and diagnostic contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{conditional empirical finding}: a deliberately minimal probe-then-act policy improves transfer on one recoverable OOD elastoplastic split (+14.7pp transfer, $-$14.7pp spill, +16.7pp success) while losing by 13--22pp on the four other splits.
    \item A \textbf{recoverability-based explanatory hypothesis}: the sign of probing's effect may depend on whether the elicited response is task-useful and the induced state change is recoverable, but this is an interpretation of the current evidence, not a proven law.
    \item A \textbf{reproducible hidden-dynamics benchmark}: five Genesis material splits, a 55pp scripted-transfer spread, standardized 3-seed evaluation for the main M1/M7 comparison, per-seed metrics, and ablation infrastructure.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
\PTA{} as evaluated here uses a fixed open-loop probe, observations that include simulator-derived aggregate-particle statistics, and an end-to-end task-conditioning latent without calibration or material-label supervision. The results support a scoped diagnostic reading: explicit probing can help in one recoverable-response setting and can hurt in several irreversible or partly irreversible settings. They do not establish broad cross-material robustness, a general superiority of probing over passive context inference, or a validated boundary law.
